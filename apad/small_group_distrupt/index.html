Large teams develop and small teams disrupt science and technology

Paper

Wu, L., Wang, D. & Evans, J.A. Large teams develop and small teams disrupt science and technology. Nature 566, 378–382 (2019). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-0941-9

Summary

Small teams tend to disrupt science and technology with new ideas and opportunities while large teams tend to develop existing one.

  1. One of the most universal trends in science and technology is that team size the growth of the team size.
  2. New Question: Whether and how the character of the scienc and technology produced by large teams differs from that of small teams
  3. Work from larger teams builds on more-recent and popular developments, and attention to their work comes immediately.
  4. Contributions by smaller teams search more deeply into the past, are viewed as disruptive to science and technology and succeed further into the future—if at all.

Main

  • Although much has been demonstrated about the professional and carrer benefits of team size for team members, there is little evidence that supports the notion that larger teams are optimized for knowledge discovery and technological invention.

  • Experimental and observational resarch on groups reveals that individual in large groups think and act differently. They generated fewer ideas, recall less learned information, reject external perspectives more often, and tend to neutralize each other’s viewpoint.

  • There was the research which demontrates that large article and patent teams reveive slightly more citations. However, citation counts alone cannot capture distinct types of contribution. (I totally agree.) Ex) BTW model vs Bose-Einstein condensation

Quantifying disruption

  • The authors built three citation networks comprising focal papers, references and subsequent work. The focal paper, which we are interested in, refer some references. Then some subsequent works may refer both focal paper and refereces that the focal paper refered (j) while some other subsequent works may refer only the focal paper (i). Also, there could be some subsequent works that refer only the references not the focal work (k).

  • Then the distruption defined as

$$ D = (n_i-n_j)/(n_i + n_j + n_k) $$

Result

  • They found out that articles, patents and softwares by small teams is subastantially more disruptive than work by large teams.

  • This tendency is magnified when the impact level of the paper is high.

  • This tendency still exits for 90% of disciplines. Engineering and computer science belong to the 10% exception.
