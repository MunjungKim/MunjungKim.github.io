A neuromarker of sustained attention from whole-brain functional connectivity

Paper

Rosenberg, M., Finn, E., Scheinost, D. et al. A neuromarker of sustained attention from whole-brain functional connectivity. Nat Neurosci 19, 165–171 (2016). https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.4179

Summary

  1. The authors identified that functional brain netoworks whose strength during a sustained attention task predicted individual differences in attentional performance.

  2. Furthermorethe model suggested in the paper also be able to predict a clinical measure of attention (ADHD) from resting state connectivity in an independent sample of children and adolsecents.

Main

  • Synchronous fluctuations in the blood oxygenation level–dependent (BOLD) signal, measured with functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), are thought to reflect functional connectivity in that they reveal regions engaging in common or related processing

  • Since attention ability requires various ability such as selecting necessary information and inhibition of unselected information, it involves a wide variety of brain regions.

  • The authors proved that the strength of FC networks predicts sustained attention.

Methodology

  • Input data are FC (Functional Connectivity) and Behavioral data. Behavioral data should spread across subjects. Divide the data into train and test data set.

  • In train data set, each element in FC is related to the behavioral data using linear regression.

  • After linear regression, the most important edges are selected using some methods. Typical method is using significance testing or cut off the data whose correlation is below specific threshold.

  • For each subject, selected elements in FC are summarized into a scalar data. Usually, data are simply summed.

  • Build a predictive model simply using linear regression with summarized brain data and behavioral data.

  • Apply the model to the test data set.

Applications of the method.

  • Various open-source data (e.g. HCP, PNC ..) can be pooled or combined with local datasets to test whether a predictive model generalizes across different scanners, different subject populations, or different measures of phenotype.

Comparison with other methods.

Advantages

  • Linear Regression Model (Which is very very very simple)
  • In the experiment, predictions using CPM are as good or better than those generated using SVR. (This is very interesting)

Disadvatages

  • Linear regression model can be not suitable for non-linear relationships between connectivity and behavior.

  • Other methods may outperform CPM in terms of prediction accuracy on certain data sets.

Therefore, researcher should carefully care cons and pros of this method and choose their priorities. Combining various methods can also be a solution.

Experiment Design

  • These methods have the most utility in studies with moderate to large sample. (N > 100)

  • The correlation between predicted values and true values are calculated as a test statistic. However, it is unclear what null distribution is associated with this statistic and thus a permutation is needed.

  • In addition to performing state-of-the-art preprocessing methods to minimize the effect from head motion, researchers need to ensure that motion is not correlated with the behavioral data.

  • Potential variables that could be correlated with the behavior of interest include age, gender or IQ. The effects of these confounding factors could be removed using partial correlation.

  • Projecting model features back into brain space facilitates interpretation based on known relationships between brain structure and function.

Visualization

  • First, choose a set of features to visualize and interpret. Good features should overlap across different iterations. The most conservative approach is to visualize only edges that were selected in all iterations of the analysis.

  • Glass brain plots, circle plots, and matrix plots.
