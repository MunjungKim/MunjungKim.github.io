Mathematical Background

Malkus-Lorenz water wheel

I referred to the class MAE5790-15at Cornell University, Modelling the Chaotic Waterwheel, and paper by Miroslav Kolar and Godfrey Gumbs.

The above figure is a discrete case of Malkus-Lorenz waterwheel. It has $N$ countable baskets, which have the same size with each other. In this case, water can not transport from one basket to another basket. ((a) in Fig.~\ref{fig:continuous})

\begin{figure}[h] \centering \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{Screen Shot 2021-05-07 at 1.10.35 AM.png} \caption{Continuous Water Wheel Model} \cite{contiuous} \label{fig:continuous} \end{figure}

The notion of a discrete wheel can expand to a continuous version. In this case, water in specific section \(d\theta\) can move to other sections such that the frame of inference is fixed outside the rotation of the wheel.

Here, I am going to express the dynamics of a waterwheel mathematically and show how the movement of a waterwheel follows Lorenz Equation

Before we start the section, let us define some notations.

  • \(w(t)\) : angular velocity of the water wheel.

  • \(\theta\) : angle in lab frame where $\theta = 0$ indicates 12:00 direction.

  • \(Q(\theta)\) : The rate at which water is pumped in. (Not depend on $t$)

  • \(r\) : radius of the wheel

  • \(m(\theta,t)\) : mass distribution of water around rim.

Conservation Law

The mass of water between \(\theta_1\) and \(\theta_2\) is

\begin{equation} M = \int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2} m(\theta,t)d\theta \end{equation}

The conservation law of the mass of water in the wheel can be written as

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial m(\theta,t)}{\partial t} = Q - Km(\theta,t) - w\frac{\partial m(\theta,t)}{\partial \theta} \end{equation}

where $Q$ is the amount of water in-flowing into the wheel and $Km$ is the amount of water leaking from the water wheel. \(w \frac{\partial m(\theta,t)}{\partial t}\) is the amount of water transport from $\theta$ to the adjacent part. Here, I assume that the rate at which water leaking from the water wheel is constant.

Then, consider the sector where \(\theta \in [\theta_1,\theta_2]\).

The mass between this section, \(M\) can be expressed as

\begin{equation} \Delta M =\Delta t \int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2} \frac{\partial m(\theta,t)}{\partial t}d\theta= \Delta t \Big[ \int^{\theta_2}_{\theta_1} Q(\theta) d\theta - \int^{\theta_2}_{\theta_1} Km(\theta) d\theta -m(\theta_2) w+m(\theta_1)w\Big] \end{equation}

Physical interpretation is as follows.

\(\Delta t \int^{\theta_2}_{\theta_1} Q(\theta) d\theta\) is the amount of water inflowing to the section \([\theta_1,\theta_2]\).

\(\Delta t \int^{\theta_2}_{\theta_1} Km(\theta) d\theta \) is the amount of water leaking from the section.

\(m(\theta_2) w \Delta t\) is the amount of water carried out to the section and \(m(\theta_1) w \Delta t\) is carried into the section.

If the section is \([0,2\pi]\)

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial M}{\partial t} = Q_{tot} -KM \end{equation}

where $M$ is the total mass of water on the wheel and $Q_tot$ is the total rate at which water is pumped into the whole wheel.

Newton’s law

Suppose \(I(t)\) is the moment of inertia of the wheel. Then, the torque can be expressed as

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial (Iw)}{\partial t} \end{equation}

There are two source terms that produce torque. One is the damping term, and the other is the torque due to gravity. If we assume that the damping torque is linear to the angular velocity, we have

\begin{equation} \frac{\partial (Iw)}{\partial t} = -vw + gr\int^{2\pi}_{0}m(\theta,t)sin\theta d\theta \end{equation}

,where \(v\) is the angular velocity of the wheel and $g$ is effective gravity.

The moment of inertia $I$ can be treated as a constant provided that enough time has elapsed and reached the stable phase. Therefore,

\begin{equation} I\frac{dw}{dt} = -vw + gr\int^{2\pi}_{0}m(\theta,t)sin\theta d\theta \end{equation}

Fourier Series

Since \(m(\theta,t)\) is \(2\pi\) periodic in $\theta$, it can be represented as a Fourier time series.

\begin{equation} m(\theta,t) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n(t)\sin(n\theta) + b_n(t)\cos(n\theta) \end{equation}

Similarly, \(Q(\theta)\) also can be represented as Fourier series.

\begin{equation} Q(\theta) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}q_n\cos(n\theta) \end{equation}

The reason why there is no sine term in $Q$ is because water falls into the wheel symmetrically at the top.

Now, we can plug Eqs.(8) and (9) into Eq.(2).

\begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n(t)\sin(n\theta) + b_n(t)\cos(n\theta) & = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}q_n\cos(n\theta)- K\Big[\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n(t)\sin(n\theta) + b_n(t)\cos(n\theta)\Big] \\ & - w\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n(t)\sin(n\theta) + b_n(t)\cos(n\theta) \end{split} \end{equation}

Since sine and cosine functions have orthogonality, we can calculate the coefficients respectively,

\begin{equation} \dot{a_n} = -Ka_n +w b_n \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \dot{b_n} = q_n-Kb_n -w a_n \end{equation}

Finally, we can plug Eq.(9) into Eq.(7).

\begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{\partial (Iw)}{\partial t} &= -vw + gr \int_{0}^{2\pi} \Big[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n(t) \sin(n\theta) + b_n(t)\cos(n\theta)\Big]\sin\theta d\theta\\ & -vw + gr \int_{0}^{2\pi} \Big[a_1(t) \sin(\theta) \Big] \sin(\theta) d\theta =-vw+gra_1\pi \end{split} \end{equation}

Here, all the terms except \(\sin\theta\) in \(m(\theta,t)\) vanish because of the orthogonality.

In summary we get three equations, where \(n\) is a non negative integer.

\begin{equation} \dot{a_n}= -Ka_n+w b_n \end{equation} \begin{equation} \dot{b_n}=q_n-Kb_n-w a_n \end{equation} \begin{equation} I\dot{w} =-vw+gra_1\pi \end{equation}

In the above equation, only \(a_1\),\(b_1\), and \(w\) are decoupled. Therefore,

\begin{equation} \dot{a_1} = -K a_1 + w b_1 \end{equation} \begin{equation} \dot{b_1} = q_1 - K b_1 - wa_1 \end{equation} \begin{equation} I\dot{w} = -vw +gra_1 \pi \end{equation}

This is exactly the same as the Lorenz equation after introducing the following equations and change of the time coordinate from \(t\) to \(s = Kt\)

$$ \sigma = \frac{v}{KI} $$

\begin{equation} \begin{split} \rho &= \frac{v}{KI}\\ \beta &= 1 \end{split} \end{equation}

and

$$ x = \frac{w}{K} $$

\begin{equation} \begin{split} y &= \frac{\rho K a_1}{q_1}\\ z &= \rho - \frac{\rho K b_1}{q_1} \end{split} \end{equation}

Lorenz Equation

Properties

  1. Lorenz equations have a symmetric pair of solutions \((x,y,z)\) and \((-x,-y,z)\)

  2. z-axis is invariant. The point on the z axis where \(x=0,y=0\) remains on it.

  3. Stationary Point. (I used \(r\) instead of \(\rho\) here)

  • For \(0<r<1 \), Lorenz equation has only one stable fixed point.

  • At \(r=1\), there is a bifurcation of a simple kind

  • For \(r>1\), the origin is non-stable. There are two other stationary points which we shall call \(C_1\) and \(C_2\).

    • For \(\frac{(\sigma + \beta + 3)\sigma}{\sigma-b-1}>r>1\), \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) are stable.

    • For \(\frac{(\sigma + \beta + 3)\sigma}{\sigma-b-1}<r\) , \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) are non-stable.
