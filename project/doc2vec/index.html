Neural Embedding of Research Papers Reveals Characteristics of Policy Research Institute

\(Munjung Kim^1, Jisung Yoon^2, Hyunuk Kim^3, Woo-Sung Jung^{1,2}\)

\(^1\)Department of Physics, Pohang University of Science and Technology; \(^2\)Department of Industrial and Management Engineering,PohangUniversity of Science and Technology; \(^3\)Questrom School of Business, Boston University

Abstract

The policy research institute plays an important role in shaping national agendas by providing evidence to policymakers. Many countries haverecently introduced a national evaluation on their research institutes, fulfilling the requirements for the optimal allocation of resources. However,for policy reports, the lack of qualitative measures hinders fair evaluations. For this purpose, several quantitative methods (e.g., topic modeling)have been proposed to overcome this issue, but they still have some limitations in that they could not characterize institutes’ research activitiesregarding diverse and rapidly changing topics. Here, we present a neural embedding approach that learns a vector representation of researchpapers as a new solution to the aforementioned issues and elucidate that this approach can determine the characteristics of each institute [1].

We source 21,397 policy research papers published from 2000 to 2018 by 27 national policy research institutes in the Republic of Korea. Eachresearch paper is represented as a single vector by doc2vec method (Fig. 1a). We visualize the embedding space by projecting embeddingvectors into a two-dimensional space and observe that the same or similar departments are well clustered (Fig. 1b). In the embedding space, themean vector of each institute’s papers can be considered the overall interest of the institute. Based on this concept, we define the diversity of thetopic in each institute’s papers as the average cosine distance from the embedding vectors of the papers to the overall interest (the mean vector ofeach institute). In addition, the temporal change of the diversity is regarded as how much the research areas of the institute have expanded orshrunk. We introduce topical change, defined as the cosine distance between the mean vectors in different years, to quantify how much overallresearch interests have shifted across periods.

According to the results, the Korean Women Development Institute (KWDI) is classified as the most generalized and expanding institutebetween 2000 and 2018. This finding is consistent with the emergence of women’s issues over the past two decades in Korea [2]. In addition, theKorean Development Institute (KDI) and Korea Information Society Development Institute (KISDI) have quickly changed their overall topics(Fig. 1e), which is compatible with the government’s appraisal report. Therefore, we conclude that our approach can help to easily grasp thecharacteristics of policy research institutes both numerically and systemically. This capability to capture the characteristics of the institutes canserve as a new quantitative analytic frame for policy research.
